Context
It all started with one line in the brief: "navigate indoor venues like a ninja." No specs, no defined users, no fixed stack — just that sentence. Seven months later, we'd shipped a social navigation platform built on the Earth's own geomagnetic field.
My responsibility
I led UX and UI for both sides of the product — the mobile app people used to find their way, and the web panel venue managers used to watch over the floor — from the first workshop all the way to dev handoff.
User problem
Picture landing in an airport, a museum, a conference hall you've never set foot in. No indoor GPS exists. The map is a static PDF pinned to a wall. And you have no idea whether the people you're looking for are two rooms away or two floors away.
What we did
We ended up building two products under one roof: a cross-platform app that finds you indoors using IndoorAtlas' geomagnetic positioning, and a real-time control panel for venue staff — with privacy woven into the foundations, not bolted on at the end.

Outcomes

Here's what changed once real people started using it, not just testers in a room:

We A/B tested minimal icon cues against text labels and found the simpler cues won by +23% in POI navigation success
9 out of 10 people reached their Point of Interest unaided — no help screen, no second look at the map
The mutual visibility system came out as the most trusted feature in every single usability session — nobody flagged it as creepy

Challenges and takeaways

I was designing for two completely different people at once: the visitor with a phone in one hand, rushing to find a room, and the admin behind a desktop screen, watching a live floor plan. Keeping both honestly served — without quietly building two separate products — was the tension I lived with for seven months.

There was a real risk that inFi would feel like a surveillance app, and no toggle buried in a settings menu was going to fix that — it had to be solved in the architecture itself. So we built mutual visibility: you only show up on someone's map if they've shared their location with you too. I held that line even when the pressure came, late in delivery, to simplify it away.

Phase 01

Research

Getting to know the problem through Users, Devs and Client Research.

When I first heard "indoor positioning," I pictured GPS with extra steps. I was wrong. The client's real vision was multiple people tracked live inside a venue, plus a social layer of messaging, friend requests, and even group games — something closer to a social network than a map.

I spent the early weeks in stakeholder workshops, digging through competing indoor-positioning SDKs, and talking to the people who actually get lost in big venues — conference attendees, museum visitors, travellers rushing through airports.

Key Learning 1
Nobody was asking me for a better map. They wanted context — where their colleagues were, what was happening nearby, how packed a room already was. The social layer wasn't a feature I invented; it was the thing they actually needed.
Key Learning 2
Regular GPS just gives up indoors. IndoorAtlas, which reads tiny variations in Earth's own magnetic field, was the only option that got us 1–3m accuracy without asking anyone to install extra hardware. That one technical fact quietly shaped everything that came after it.
Key Learning 3
Venue managers and venue visitors wanted almost opposite things, on completely different devices, at completely different moments. One product had to respect both — without one experience quietly winning at the other's expense.

Phase 02

Define

inFi had to speak to two audiences with nothing in common except the same map underneath — different devices, different timeframes, different ideas of what success even looked like.

The research surfaced a clear tension: first-time visitors wanted to orient themselves fast and connect with people they knew — without feeling watched. Venue admins wanted real-time occupancy and a way to reach a crowd of visitors at once, from a completely different device, at a completely different moment. Any solution had to serve both honestly, without quietly becoming two separate products.

Persona 01
The First-Timer
Role: Conference attendee

Just wants to find room B3, spot a colleague, and see what's happening nearby — all within the first 5 minutes of walking in. Hates feeling watched.

"I don't want to feel watched. I just want to find my colleague and get to my session without wasting the first ten minutes lost."
Persona 02
The Venue Manager
Role: Hotel / event ops lead

Watches floor occupancy in real time, sends messages to everyone at once, and needs a capacity alert before things get out of hand — all from a desktop browser, mid-event.

"I need to see the whole floor at a glance, and reach everyone at once the moment something changes."
Persona 03
The Social Connector
Role: Regular venue user

Wants to see where their friends are, send them a message, and walk toward them — without broadcasting their own location to every stranger in the building.

"I want to see where my friends are — but only if they've chosen to share that with me too."

Phase 03

Ideate

Can I avoid building a surveillance tool?

Real-time location sharing is inFi's core value — but the same feature that makes it useful makes it feel like a tracking app. I pushed back on the default "opt-in once, always visible" model and ran structured brainstorming to find something better.

  • 01The tension: Real-time location sharing is the product's core value, but it's also what makes an app feel like surveillance if handled carelessly.
  • 02The ideation: Generated alternative privacy models — asymmetric visibility, proximity-only sharing, time-limited presence — and evaluated each on an impact/privacy-risk matrix.
  • 03Mutual visibility wins: You only appear on someone's map if they've also shared their location with you. This became a product principle, not just a setting.
Keeping Admins Happy Too

For venue admins, we scoped tools to make their side of the product just as useful: bulk messaging to reach a crowd at once, and a real-time view of traffic overlaid on the floor plan — without turning it into surveillance of individual visitors.

Phase 04

Prototype

Turning ideas into something we could actually put in front of people.

I started with low-fi wireframes, keeping everything simple so we could focus on how things worked, not how they looked. Mapping out interactions and catching errors early saved us tons of time later. Since we were building on IndoorAtlas, we chose the Ionic Framework for the mobile app — ideal for a cross-platform build — and the Foundation Framework for the admin side, to keep things stable and responsive under real data.

Using Figma, I built clickable prototypes and handed them to stakeholders to try on their own devices. Their feedback was directly responsible for refining the design and fixing hiccups before any of it reached development.

Decision caught at mid-fi — first-person view cut

A first-person perspective view, inspired by FPS games, had been on the feature list from the start. Once we built it out in mid-fi, it was obvious it added real technical risk for almost no navigation benefit. We cut it before any hi-fi work began — saving development time and keeping the scope honest.

Phase 05

Test

A clickable prototype tells you if a flow makes sense on paper. It doesn't tell you if a stranger in an airport can actually use it.

I ran sessions with real people in realistic scenarios — navigating an unfamiliar airport, exploring a museum floor they'd never seen, checking into a venue cold. The core navigation held up: people moved through the map without much hesitation. But two things came back consistently: first-time users needed clearer onboarding, and playful touches like the proximity compass weren't just decoration — they made people want to keep exploring, not just tolerate the app until they found what they needed.

What testing caught early

The mutual visibility switch tested as a clear win — every session flagged it as the thing that made people feel safe, not watched. But first-time navigation without an onboarding pass was shakier than expected, and font sizing broke down in the dim, ambient lighting of real venues. Both went straight back into the design queue instead of shipping as-is.

Version A · Text labels for POIs

Informative, but added visual noise to an already dense map — users hesitated reading labels instead of just walking.

Winner · Version B · Minimal icon cues + helper text

Quieter map, faster recognition. Lifted successful, unaided POI navigation by 23% — this is why the final map leans on iconography over walls of text.

Phase 06

Design

Making it beautiful, easy to use, and ready for teams I'd never meet to build.

The client gave me one clear directive: FC Bayern red on a dark palette. That single constraint ended up shaping a whole visual language — dark enough to read comfortably in a dim conference hall, structured enough to survive a dense admin dashboard too. I followed Steve Krug's golden rule, "don't make me think": if you didn't have to stop and think, I knew I'd done my job.

Phase 07

Handoff

The real gap in any project isn't between the mockup and the code — it's between the designer who made it and the ten other teams, in ten other cities, who'll build it without ever meeting her.

Everything changed the moment the client told us inFi would be resold and rebuilt by other teams around the world. A deck of colors, typography and component states was fine for a team that shared a coffee machine — it was not going to survive being handed to developers I'd never talk to.

So I mapped out a proper pattern library: every component's attributes, typography, colors and states, organized by usage hierarchy. Then, since "email a PDF and hope for the best" isn't a real handoff strategy, I used a plugin that turned every artboard into an interactive HTML document — developers could click through each screen and read exactly what they needed before writing a line of code.

Designing for teams I'd never meet

This is the part of the project that mirrors how I like to work now: documentation and specs precise enough that a distributed team can build from them without a sync call. The interactive styleguide wasn't a nice-to-have — it was the difference between "the designer is a bottleneck" and "the designer wrote it down once, correctly."

What I'd do differently: I underestimated, early on, how differently a visitor with a phone in one hand and an admin behind a desktop screen would need to be designed for. Keeping both honestly served, without quietly building two separate products, was the tension I lived with for seven months — and I'd bring that persona split into the room a lot sooner next time.

Where I held the line

It would have been much easier to ship a simple "always visible" toggle and call it done — that's exactly the app nobody wants to open in a crowded venue. Holding the line on "you only appear if they've shared too," even under late-stage pressure to simplify it away, is what made the social layer feel safe instead of creepy. That's the moment I'm most proud of on this project: not the pixels, the decision.

Shipped Features

The underlying principle

I still believe the best navigation design is invisible. If someone finds who they're looking for and can't quite explain how they did it, the design worked. Every decision here — the proximity compass, the mutual visibility model, all of it — was in service of that one idea.
