TL;DR

What we built

A direct-manipulation touch EQ interface inside an Android app that remote-controls the Behringer X32 — replacing six overloaded knobs as the fastest way to shape a channel.

How we built it

Field research at live venues and studios → two competing layout concepts → A/B testing with real audio engineers → handoff with annotated Figma specs for the Android engineering team.

What landed

85% user satisfaction post-launch · −30% time on task for EQ adjustments · 70% adoption rate — and an interface that engineers could trust on stage.

Context
The X32 runs every parameter through six rotating encoders, several doing double or triple duty. MusicTribe wanted an Android app that gave engineers a full touch alternative — without breaking the workflow they already trusted.
User problem
Six overloaded knobs meant engineers had to remember each one's current mode before trusting what it would do — often in the dark, mid-show. The most friction-heavy task on the console.
My responsibility
Led product design end-to-end — field research, ideation, A/B-tested prototyping, and handoff to the mobile engineering team.
What we did
Designed a direct-manipulation touch EQ graph and a precision-lock gesture for fine adjustments — validated through A/B testing two layouts with real audio engineers.

Outcomes

85% user satisfaction score post-launch, based on surveys and direct feedback from engineers
−30% average time on task for EQ adjustments, measured in timed usability sessions
70% adoption rate for the new remote app, tracked through app usage analytics post-launch

Challenges and takeaways

Precision under live conditions. Standard mobile touch-target sizing wasn't enough for stressed hands mid-show — hit areas and gesture logic took two extra iterations to get right.

Respecting 30 years of muscle memory. The X32's knob-and-button workflow runs deep. The app had to feel like an extension of that world, not a replacement — or adoption would stall at zero.

Phase 01

Empathize

Spending time where audio professionals actually work — live venues and recording studios.

The X32 lives in two worlds: the chaos of a live show and the precision of a studio. I started by observing engineers in both — watching where they hesitated and what workarounds they'd invented.

I backed that up with interviews and a review of existing feedback logs — reading the console's history of frustration as a design brief.

Field Research

On-site at live venues and studios, watching engineers work the console under real time pressure.

Feedback Analysis

Reviewed interaction logs and user feedback, mapping failure patterns against console sections.

The starting point: six rotating encoders below a small display, covering Lo Cut, Gain, Freq, Q and Mode across four bands — quietly changing job depending on what you pressed last.

What We Kept Hearing

EQ adjustment was the most friction-heavy task on the console — precise tweaks made with tiny knobs, often in the dark, mid-performance. The hardware was getting in the way of the music.

Phase 02

Define

Translating research into a focused design goal — and understanding who we were really designing for.

The research surfaced a tension: experienced engineers had muscle memory tied to physical controls, while newer users found the interface dense. Any touch solution had to serve both.

Two pain points stood out — EQ adjustment under pressure, and navigating between channels quickly. Both pointed to the same need: faster access to the right control at the right moment.

Marco · Live Sound Engineer

Sara · Studio Musician

Phase 03

Ideate

Sketching new interaction models for a physical device with decades of established conventions.

Designing an Android app to remote-control existing hardware meant real constraints — a 1024×768 canvas, and years of muscle memory built around six overloaded encoders. The brief's early mockup called for one physical rotary knob for fine adjustment — a "touch and turn" hybrid. A tablet has no dial to inherit, so I translated that into touch alone: a long-press-and-drag gesture that slows down for surgical moves.

The core idea: a touch-enabled EQ graph. Instead of hunting through knob modes, the engineer drags one of six points — Low Cut, Low, Low Mid, High Mid, High, High Cut — across a live curve (20Hz–20kHz frequency, −15 to +15 gain). One gesture instead of six knobs.

  • →Touch EQ graph: Direct manipulation of frequency and gain via draggable nodes across all six bands — the core of the interface.
  • →Precision lock: A long-press-and-drag gesture stands in for the physical dial the brief called for — slows touch response for fine adjustments.
  • →Tab bar navigation: A persistent Home / Config / Gate / Dyn / EQ / Sends / Main bar keeps every mode one tap away.
  • →Global controls: Mix blend (0–150%), Solo, Reset, and one unmistakable EQ on/off toggle — always visible, always reachable without hunting.
  • →Preset system: Save and recall custom EQ configurations with a single tap.
  • →Visual indicators: Clear signifiers for the active band, on/off state, and which parameter is locked for precision adjustment.
The Controls Panel Question

Early sketches produced two layout approaches for the controls panel — a decision left to A/B testing, not assumption.

Version A · Controls at top

Rationale: Maximum visibility of parameters on load. Follows conventional settings-panel patterns.

Version B · Controls at bottom

Rationale: Optimised for thumb reach and one-handed use, where fingers naturally rest.

The brief's own early mockup: the EQ touch area, six draggable points, a parameter field per band, and a note about a physical "touch and turn" knob — later translated into a touch-only gesture for the tablet.

Two requirements shaped every pixel: touch targets sized for a stressed thumb in a dim room, legible from a working distance — and everything vector-based, so the component library could be reused across future mobile and desktop apps.

Phase 04

Prototype

From sketches to a high-fidelity Figma prototype — testing interaction logic before it touched hardware.

I moved through fidelity levels deliberately: paper sketches for information architecture, then mid-fi Figma wireframes to stress-test layout density — the EQ graph needed room to work without crowding the screen.

The high-fi prototype covered three key workflows: EQ adjustment, preset management, and channel navigation — the highest-frequency, highest-stakes tasks in a live environment.

Prototype-Driven Decision

Mid-fi testing showed EQ node handles needed to be bigger than standard touch targets — live hands are imprecise. Final hit areas: 44×44pt minimum, with magnetic snapping to guide the gesture.

Phase 05

Test

A/B testing and hands-on usability sessions with real audio engineers — measuring what mattered.

Testing centred on the tasks that defined success in the field: EQ speed, control discoverability, and channel navigation — with both experienced engineers and less technical musicians.

  • 01A/B layout test: Both versions tested with identical tasks; time-on-task and satisfaction recorded for each.
  • 02EQ manipulation accuracy: Users hit a target frequency and gain via the touch graph; time and corrections needed were measured.
  • 03Preset recall: Engineers saved and recalled a custom EQ profile mid-session. Discoverability of the save function was a common early failure.
  • 04Channel navigation: Cross-channel switching was timed across both layouts to identify where friction remained.
Version A · Controls at top

Quick to adapt on first load, but users reported fatigue over longer sessions — reaching upward repeatedly got uncomfortable.

✓ Winner · Version B · Controls at bottom

Clear favourite in feedback — ergonomically natural standing or seated, with lower time-on-task across every workflow.

Key Finding — Preset Discoverability

Preset save/recall was consistently missed early on — users expected it next to the EQ graph, not in a separate panel. We moved it, and first-attempt success jumped.

Phase 06

Deliver

Final UI, design handoff documentation, and the visual decisions that carried the interface across the finish line.

The final interface built on Version B's layout, refined through two more rounds of iteration. The touch EQ graph is the centrepiece — large and directly responsive, with parameter fields anchored at the bottom within thumb reach.

Visual decisions were deliberate: a dark, high-contrast palette for low-light venues, with colour — not text — marking states like active, engaged, and clipping. Readable at a glance.

Handoff docs for the Android engineering team covered every interactive state, touch target dimension, and edge case — especially EQ behaviour at frequency extremes and multi-node editing.

EQ Graph Behaviour

Drag a node to move frequency and gain together. Long-press for precision lock. Tap Reset for defaults. No pinch or multi-finger gestures — live hands don't have room for those.

Cross-Channel Navigation

Swipe left/right between channels without losing EQ view state. Channel strip thumbnails in the header give spatial context and quick jump targets.

Phase 07

Results & Learnings

What shipped, what we measured, and what I'd approach differently.

85%
User satisfaction score post-launch (surveys + direct feedback)
−30%
Average time on task for EQ adjustments (timed usability tests)
70%
Adoption rate for the new remote app (in-app analytics)

What I'm most proud of: the touch EQ graph. Obvious in hindsight, but getting it precise enough for pros, fast enough for live work, and accessible enough for newcomers took real iteration — the A/B data confirmed we got there.

What I'd do differently: I underestimated how much the testing environment matters. A studio chair doesn't capture the reality of standing at a console mid-show — less stable hands, divided attention, unpredictable light. Earlier in-context testing would have surfaced the touch target issue sooner.

The underlying principle

The best remote UI is invisible. If an engineer adjusts three bands mid-set and can't quite explain how — they just grabbed the sound — the design worked.
