The Starting Problem

The brief was deliberately open: "build something for navigating indoor venues like a ninja." No specs, no target users, no KPIs. Our job was to turn that into a product — fast.

Phase 01

Empathize

Understanding the space, the technology, and the humans we were designing for.

The first thing I did was challenge my assumptions. "Indoor navigation" sounded like GPS — simple. But a week of discovery revealed a completely different landscape.

GPS signals are blocked indoors. The dominant alternative was IndoorAtlas, a Finnish platform that uses Earth's geomagnetic field variations to triangulate position inside buildings — accurate to 1–3 metres without any extra hardware.

I ran stakeholder workshops with the client and combined them with competitive analysis and exploratory user interviews with people who regularly navigate complex venues: conference attendees, museum visitors, airport travellers.

Key Insight from Research

Users didn't just want a map. They wanted context — where their colleagues were, what events were happening nearby, how crowded an area was. The social layer wasn't a feature request; it was the underlying need.

Technology Discovery

Evaluated 3 indoor positioning SDKs. IndoorAtlas won on accuracy, hardware-free setup, and developer documentation quality. This constrained our tech stack to Ionic Framework for cross-platform support.

Stakeholder Alignment

The client (a sports industry executive) brought a strong brand directive: FC Bayern Munich colors — red and dark — as non-negotiable. This became the seed of the visual identity.

Phase 02

Define

Translating research into a sharp problem statement and two distinct user groups.

The research surfaced a critical tension: inFi had two very different audiences — end users navigating a venue in real time, and venue admins managing their space and attendees. Both had legitimate, conflicting needs.

I built out personas, journey maps, and a defined problem statement that would anchor every design decision that followed.

Key Design Decision — Privacy by Design

The risk of inFi feeling like a surveillance tool was real. I proposed a mutual visibility model: you only appear on another user's map if they've also shared their location with you. This became a core product principle, not just a setting.

"How might we help first-time visitors orient themselves and connect with others in complex indoor venues — without sacrificing their sense of privacy and control?" Problem statement · inFi, 2020

Phase 03

Ideate

Generating solutions through structured brainstorming, then converging on the right architecture.

With two distinct products to design — the mobile app for users and the web control panel for admins — I structured ideation separately for each surface, then looked for shared patterns.

For the mobile app, I drew inspiration from navigation software (bird's eye and first-person views), FPS game interfaces for spatial orientation, and — deliberately — dating apps for the social discovery layer. If people are comfortable swiping through nearby strangers on Tinder, we could apply that same mental model to finding colleagues in a conference hall.

  • →Navigation flows: User enters venue → sees real-time map → searches or browses POIs → sets destination → follows turn-by-turn guidance.
  • →Social flows: Toggle visibility → see nearby users → send connection request → chat or navigate toward each other.
  • →Admin flows: Monitor live floor plan → view KPIs → send targeted messages → manage capacity limits.
  • →Proximity compass: A gamified radar UI showing nearby opted-in users — inspired by FPS minimap conventions.
A Deliberate Naming Decision

We named the app inFi — a blend of "indoor" and "fidelity." The logo evolved from paper sketches to a lettermark where the dot of the i and a location pin connect with a navigation path, embedding the product metaphor directly into the identity.

Phase 04

Prototype

From paper sketches to interactive Figma prototypes — keeping fidelity low until the logic was solid.

I started with paper wireframes. This isn't a formality — low fidelity forces you to focus on interaction logic rather than visual polish. It's much cheaper to discover a navigation dead-end on paper than in code.

Once the flows were validated internally, I moved to mid-fi wireframes in Figma, building component-by-component with placeholder content. At this stage I could test the real layout density of the indoor map with live user icons, POI markers, and navigation overlays all competing for space.

For the admin panel I chose the Foundation Framework — its responsive grid was the right tool for the data-heavy dashboard with live KPIs, floor plan display, and messaging tools.

The hi-fi clickable prototype was handed directly to stakeholders to test on their personal devices. Seeing the proximity compass move in real time changed the conversation — it went from an abstract feature to something people immediately wanted to play with.

Prototype-Driven Scope Decision

During mid-fi prototyping, we realised the first-person perspective view (a sci-fi-inspired feature) was technically risky and added minimal navigation value. It was cut before any hi-fi work began — saving significant dev time.

Phase 05

Test

Usability sessions in realistic environments, plus A/B testing on key decision points.

We recruited 8 participants across two rounds — a mix of frequent venue visitors (conferences, museums) and venue staff. Testing happened in a realistic environment: a simulated conference hall setup, not a lab.

  • 01Navigation intuitiveness: Most users navigated independently. The main failure point was the onboarding — users who skipped it struggled to understand the mutual visibility model on first use.
  • 02Proximity compass: Consistently the most-commented feature. Users found it delightful and intuitive — the game-inspired metaphor transferred immediately.
  • 03POI guidance (A/B test): We tested icon-only cues vs. icon + contextual text labels. Text-labelled cues improved successful POI navigation by +23%.
  • 04Legibility in low light: Several users noted the default font size was too small for dimly lit event spaces. Iterated to a larger base size with higher contrast.
  • 05Admin tooltips: First-time admin users couldn't interpret KPI toggles without guidance. Added progressive disclosure tooltips on first login.
Biggest Failure Caught in Testing

The initial onboarding assumed users understood geomagnetic positioning. They didn't — and they weren't interested in the explanation. We replaced the technical intro with a benefit-led flow: "See who's nearby. Share your location to connect."

Phase 06

Design

From tested wireframes to a polished, documented design system — for both surfaces.

inFi had a genuinely rare design challenge: it had to feel like a navigation tool and a social app simultaneously, without feeling like either was bolted on. The visual language had to carry both.

The color system anchored to the client brief — FC Bayern-inspired red as the primary action color — but I pushed the overall palette toward a dark, map-like aesthetic that referenced cartography software and felt premium in low-light environments.

For the admin panel, the design language shifted: lighter, more data-dense, built on a structured grid. The same design token system underpinned both products, ensuring consistency without visual monotony.

Mobile App Principles

Dark-first. Map as canvas. People as primary content. Navigation iconography borrowed from established wayfinding systems (exit signs, accessibility markers) for instant recognition — no learning curve.

Admin Panel Principles

Information density over decoration. Real-time data legibility at a glance. Progressive disclosure for advanced features (tooltips, contextual help) to reduce overwhelm for first-time admins.

Design System Approach

I documented all components with their states (default, hover, active, disabled, loading) in a living style guide. When the client later wanted to scale inFi to additional venues, this documentation was what made handoff to a new agency seamless.

Phase 07

Results & Learnings

What we shipped, what we measured, and what I'd do differently.

POI navigation success rate after A/B test iteration
90%
Target task success rate for unaided navigation
7mo
From blank brief to deployed product on both platforms
<5%
Target navigation error rate in venue testing

What I'm most proud of: the mutual visibility system. It's easy to add a privacy toggle as an afterthought. Making it a foundational product principle — something that shaped the entire social graph — required convincing stakeholders early and holding the line through pressure to simplify. The testing data confirmed it: it was the feature users trusted most.

What I'd do differently: I underestimated how technically novel the geomagnetic positioning was for users. A richer, context-appropriate onboarding — not just a feature tour but genuine expectation-setting about how the technology works and when it might be imprecise — would have reduced early drop-off.

I'd also invest earlier in a proper design token system. The style guide we built was good, but it was built after the hi-fi screens. If I were starting again, tokens first — screens second.
