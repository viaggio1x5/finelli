The Brief

Quandoo's Business Center — a web app used daily by restaurant owners to manage reservations, track performance, and communicate with customers — had grown into something that actively slowed people down. We were asked to redesign it. Not cosmetically. Fundamentally.

Phase 01

Empathize

Before designing anything, we needed to understand what it actually felt like to run a restaurant — and to fight with a tool that was supposed to help.

I started with contextual observation sessions — watching restaurant managers interact with the existing app during actual service hours, not in a lab. This distinction matters. A feature that looks fine in a usability session can be completely unusable when you have six tables waiting and a phone ringing.

What I saw: managers performing workarounds they didn't even realise were workarounds. Writing reservation notes on paper because the app's notes field was buried. Calling staff instead of using the messaging feature because finding it took too many steps. These are invisible problems — users don't report them, they adapt around them.

I followed observations with in-depth interviews with owners, managers, and floor staff, asking about goals (not features) and frustrations (not bugs). The patterns that emerged were consistent across every venue size and type.

Navigation as a maze

Users couldn't build a mental model of the app. Every task required trial and error, even for experienced users.

Data without meaning

The dashboard showed numbers, but not insights. Owners couldn't answer the question: "Is today going well?"

Reservations under pressure

The booking management interface demanded too many clicks during exactly the moments when speed mattered most.

No personalisation

Every restaurant is different. The app treated them all identically — forcing workflows that didn't fit anyone.

Insight that Changed the Direction

Restaurant owners weren't primarily asking for better features. They were asking for time back. Every inefficiency in the app was costing them minutes that should have been spent with customers. This reframed our success metric: not "did users find the feature" but "how much faster can they complete their core tasks."

Phase 02

Define

The Business Center served multiple people simultaneously — and they had fundamentally different needs, time horizons, and definitions of success.

One of the most important decisions in this project was refusing to design for "the restaurant owner" as if that were one person. A floor manager during dinner service has nothing in common with an owner reviewing the month's revenue, or a staff member checking today's schedule. Same product, completely different contexts.

I built three personas — not as decorative deliverables, but as active decision tools. Every time we debated a design choice, we asked: which persona does this serve first? And which does it slow down?

Key Decision — Role-Aware Navigation

We defined that the default landing view should adapt based on role: managers land on reservations, owners land on the dashboard. This wasn't a personalisation feature. It was a structural acknowledgment that the product serves multiple people — and treating them the same was the original sin.

Phase 03

Ideate

Every solution was anchored to a specific research finding. Nothing was added because it sounded good.

I structured ideation around the three core failure areas identified in research, rather than doing open-ended feature brainstorming. This kept the team focused and made it easier to evaluate ideas against actual user pain points.

  • 01Navigation redesign: Flattened the information architecture from 4 levels deep to 2. Introduced a persistent left-nav with role-aware defaults. Mapped out every task flow and counted clicks — nothing that required more than 3 steps to complete survived the cut.
  • 02Dashboard rethink: Instead of showing all available metrics, we defined a hierarchy of "must-know vs. nice-to-know" data together with the operations team. The resulting dashboard surface exposed the 5 most actionable metrics immediately, with full data one level down.
  • 03Reservation system overhaul: Replaced the static table list with a drag-and-drop calendar interface. Allowed inline editing of booking details without opening a separate view. Colour coding mapped to booking status — not a stylistic choice, but a functional one.
  • 04Data control: Introduced filterable views and saveable report configurations, so each user could shape the data to their context rather than interpreting a generic output.
Where Brainstorming Surprised Us

We initially assumed the main problem was visual — a cluttered interface. What emerged in ideation was that the deeper issue was information architecture. Cleaning up the visuals without restructuring the nav would have been cosmetic surgery on a structural problem. We almost made that mistake.

Phase 04

Prototype

Fidelity escalation is a discipline, not a preference. Low-fi forces honest conversations.

I started with hand-drawn wireframes for the three core areas: dashboard, reservation calendar, and navigation structure. The goal was to test the logic of each solution before investing a single hour in Figma.

At this stage, we ran concept tests with 4 merchants — showing paper sketches, not mockups. This is deliberate: when something looks rough, people tell you it doesn't work. When it looks polished, they assume they're missing something.

The paper round surfaced a critical issue with the drag-and-drop reservation calendar: on tablets (which many floor managers use), the drag targets were too small and the gesture conflicted with scroll. We redesigned the interaction model before building a single screen.

Mid-fi wireframes in Figma followed, with a strong focus on information density — a B2B tool for people doing real work needs to show more on screen, not less. The instinct to "keep it clean" can be actively harmful when your user needs to compare 12 reservations at a glance.

Decision Caught Early — Tablet Interaction Model

The drag-and-drop reservation interface worked on desktop but failed on tablets due to gesture conflicts. Caught at paper prototype stage. We added a tap-to-select + tap-to-place alternative interaction — doubling the implementation cost but serving the actual device context of our users.

Phase 05

Test

Usability testing in restaurant environments. A/B tests on specific decision points. Timed task analysis for the core workflows.

We ran two rounds of moderated usability testing: an early round on mid-fi wireframes, and a final round on the hi-fi prototype. Participants were drawn from three restaurant types — fast casual, fine dining, and high-volume venue — because the workflows differ significantly.

For timed task analysis, we defined the three most business-critical tasks and measured completion time against the legacy system. This gave us concrete before/after comparisons rather than subjective impressions.

  • 01Navigation: Task completion rate improved from ~60% to 89% after the restructured navigation. Users no longer had to backtrack to find the features they used daily.
  • 02Dashboard comprehension: When asked "is this a good evening so far?" owners could answer correctly in under 8 seconds with the new dashboard. With the legacy version, most couldn't answer at all — they needed to cross-reference multiple screens.
  • 03Reservation creation (A/B test): Tested inline editing vs. side-panel editing for reservation details. Inline editing was 34% faster to complete and strongly preferred by floor managers. Side panel was retained as a secondary mode for complex bookings.
  • 04Colour coding on the calendar: Users immediately understood booking status from colour alone — the mental model transferred from traffic lights. Validation that a familiar metaphor beats a custom system, even in a niche tool.
Biggest Failure Caught in Testing

The "smart dashboard" initially surfaced different metrics depending on time of day (lunch vs. dinner service). Users found this deeply disorienting — they needed the interface to be stable, not clever. We reverted to a consistent layout and added a manual context switch instead. Predictability beat intelligence.

Phase 06

Design

B2B design has a different brief than consumer design. Delight means saving someone 20 minutes, not a satisfying animation.

The visual language of the Business Center had to communicate authority and clarity, not approachability or warmth. Restaurant owners are professional operators. A tool that feels like it belongs in a back office is more trustworthy than one that looks like a consumer app.

I built a lean design token system first — colour, spacing, typography — and documented every component with its states (default, hover, active, loading, error, empty state) before touching screen layouts. This discipline paid off in handoff: the development team had zero ambiguity about how any component should behave.

The reservation calendar got special attention. It needed to carry significant information density — multiple bookings per slot, status indicators, customer tags — without becoming illegible. I ran multiple density tests, adjusting row height, font size, and colour weight until the floor manager persona could scan a full evening at a glance.

Design System as Handoff Strategy

Documenting components with all states — not just the happy path — reduced developer questions during implementation by a significant margin. Every "what should this look like when it's empty?" or "what happens during loading?" was answered in the documentation before code was written.

Phase 07

Results & Learnings

Three metrics that validated the approach — and two learnings that will shape the next project.

+15%
Increase in bookings, tracked via internal booking data post-launch
30%
Faster operational task completion, confirmed by timed usability analysis
+40%
User satisfaction improvement, measured via post-launch surveys

What drove the booking increase: The 15% uplift came specifically from the reservation flow redesign. Fewer steps to create and modify bookings meant staff accepted more reservations rather than deferring them. The system got out of the way, and the humans did what humans do well.

What I'd do differently: We should have involved the operations team earlier in defining the dashboard metric hierarchy. Their knowledge of which numbers actually drive decisions was invaluable — but we only brought them in at the ideation phase. Earlier involvement would have saved us one full prototype round.

I'd also invest earlier in empty state design. It was the last thing we designed and nearly didn't make it into the handoff. In a tool people use on day one — before any data exists — empty states are often the very first thing a new user sees.

The Underlying Principle

The best B2B design is invisible. Not because it lacks craft — but because the user's attention belongs on their restaurant, not on the tool. If they finish a task and don't remember how they did it, the design worked.
