The Starting Point

The Behringer X32 is a reference-class mixing console — but its interface was designed around physical knobs and buttons. The challenge: integrate a touchscreen without undermining the tactile workflow that professionals rely on, and make the most technical parts of the console genuinely accessible.

Phase 01

Empathize

Spending time where audio professionals actually work — live venues and recording studios.

The X32 lives in two very different environments: the controlled chaos of a live concert and the focused precision of a recording studio. I started by observing audio engineers in both contexts, watching how they moved across the console during performance, where hesitation happened, and what workarounds they'd invented for the interface's shortcomings.

We supplemented field observations with in-depth interviews and a review of existing user feedback logs — effectively reading the console's history of frustration as a design brief.

Field Research

Embedded observation at live venues and studios. Watched engineers navigate the console under real time pressure — multiple channels, fast transitions, no margin for error.

Feedback Analysis

Reviewed interaction logs and existing user feedback as structured data. Identified recurring failure patterns and mapped them against console sections.

What We Kept Hearing

EQ adjustment was the most friction-heavy task on the console. Engineers were doing precise, continuous parameter tweaks — gain, frequency, Q-factor — using tiny physical knobs, often in dim lighting, often mid-performance. The hardware was getting in the way of the music.

Phase 02

Define

Translating research into a focused design goal — and understanding who we were really designing for.

The research surfaced a clear tension: experienced engineers had deeply ingrained muscle memory tied to physical controls, while newer users were often overwhelmed by the density of the interface. Any touchscreen solution needed to serve both without compromising either.

The two most critical pain points emerged as: the complexity of EQ adjustment in high-pressure situations, and the difficulty of quickly surfacing and navigating between controls across multiple channels. Both pointed to the same underlying need — faster access to the right control at the right moment.

Marco · Live Sound Engineer

"During a show I don't have time to hunt for a control. If I can't find it in two seconds, I move on and something in the mix suffers."

Sara · Studio Musician

"I know what I want to hear, but I can never remember which knob does what. I want to touch the sound directly, not translate it through hardware."

Phase 03

Ideate

Sketching new interaction models for a physical device with decades of established conventions.

Designing a touchscreen for an existing hardware product meant working within strict constraints — form factor, existing control layout, user expectations from years of muscle memory. Rather than trying to replace the physical interface, I focused on augmenting it: the touchscreen as a precision layer on top of the hardware, not a replacement for it.

The central concept that emerged was the touch-enabled EQ graph: instead of turning knobs to adjust equalisation parameters, the engineer could directly grab and drag points on a visual frequency curve. It made an invisible signal visible and manipulable in a single gesture.

  • →Touch EQ graph: Direct manipulation of frequency, gain, and Q-factor via draggable nodes on a live curve — the core innovation of the interface.
  • →Channel navigation: Swipe-based channel switching to move quickly across the mix without interrupting the current workflow.
  • →Preset system: Save and recall custom EQ configurations with a single tap — critical for engineers working across multiple events.
  • →Visual indicators: Clear signifiers for active controls — gain level, frequency band, Q width — to reduce the mental translation between gesture and result.
The Controls Panel Question

Early sketching produced two distinct layout approaches for the parameter controls panel — a decision that would go directly to A/B testing rather than being resolved by assumption.

Version A · Controls at top

Rationale: Maximum immediate visibility of parameters on screen load. Follows conventional UI patterns for settings panels.

Version B · Controls at bottom

Rationale: Optimised for thumb reach during one-handed operation. Controls placed where fingers naturally rest on a surface-mounted screen.

Phase 04

Prototype

From sketches to a high-fidelity Figma prototype — testing interaction logic before it touched hardware.

I moved through fidelity levels deliberately. Low-fidelity paper sketches validated the basic information architecture and gesture model without the distraction of visual polish. Once the core flows were internally consistent, I built mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma to stress-test layout density — the EQ graph needed to be large enough for accurate touch manipulation without crowding the rest of the interface.

The high-fidelity prototype brought the full experience to life across three key workflows: adjusting EQ parameters in real time, managing presets, and navigating between channels. These scenarios were chosen because they represented the highest-frequency, highest-stakes tasks in a live environment.

Prototype-Driven Decision

During mid-fi prototyping, we realised that the EQ node handles needed to be significantly larger than standard touch targets — live performance means imprecise touches from stressed hands. The final hit areas were sized to 44×44pt minimum, with magnetic snapping to help guide imprecise gestures onto the correct node.

Phase 05

Test

A/B testing and hands-on usability sessions with real audio engineers — measuring what mattered.

Testing was structured around the two layout variants and centred on the tasks that defined success in the field: EQ adjustment speed, control discoverability, and cross-channel navigation. Participants included both experienced audio engineers and less technical musicians — the full range of our target users.

  • 01A/B layout test: Both versions were tested with identical task sets. User performance (time on task) and satisfaction (post-session rating) were recorded for each variant.
  • 02EQ manipulation accuracy: Users were asked to reach a target frequency and gain value using the touch graph. We measured time and number of corrections needed.
  • 03Preset recall: Engineers tested saving a custom EQ profile and recalling it mid-session. Discoverability of the save function was a common early failure point.
  • 04Channel navigation: Cross-channel switching was timed across both layouts to identify where friction remained.
Version A · Controls at top

Users adapted quickly on first load but reported fatigue during extended sessions — reaching upward repeatedly on a console-mounted screen was physically uncomfortable.

✓ Winner · Version B · Controls at bottom

Overwhelming preference in qualitative feedback. Ergonomically natural for both standing engineers and seated studio work. Time-on-task scores significantly lower across all tested workflows.

Key Finding — Preset Discoverability

The save/recall function for custom presets was consistently missed in early test sessions. Users expected it adjacent to the EQ graph, not in a separate panel. We relocated it in the next iteration — and first-attempt success rate jumped significantly.

Phase 06

Deliver

Final UI, design handoff documentation, and the visual decisions that carried the interface across the finish line.

The final interface was built around Version B's ergonomic layout, refined through two further rounds of iteration following the test findings. The touch EQ graph became the centrepiece of the screen — large, precise, and directly responsive, with the parameter controls (gain, frequency, Q) anchored at the bottom within comfortable thumb reach.

Visual decisions were deliberate: a dark, high-contrast palette matched the low-light conditions of live venues. Key control states — active, engaged, clipping — used colour distinctively rather than relying on text labels. The goal was readability at a glance, not after parsing.

Handoff documentation was prepared for the engineering team with annotated Figma specs covering all interactive states, touch target dimensions, and edge cases — particularly for the EQ graph behaviour at frequency extremes and during multi-node editing.

EQ Graph Behaviour

Direct drag to move frequency nodes. Long-press to isolate a band. Pinch to adjust Q-width. Double-tap node to reset to default. All gestures mapped to established touch conventions — no new learning required.

Cross-Channel Navigation

Swipe left/right to move between channels without losing current EQ view state. Channel strip thumbnails in the header provide spatial context and quick jump targets across the full mix.

Phase 07

Results & Learnings

What shipped, what we measured, and what I'd approach differently.

85%
User satisfaction score post-launch (surveys + direct feedback)
−30%
Average time on task for EQ adjustments (timed usability tests)
70%
Adoption rate for the new touchscreen interface (console analytics)

What I'm most proud of: the touch EQ graph. It sounds obvious in retrospect — of course you should be able to grab a frequency curve directly — but translating that into a design that's precise enough for professional work, fast enough for live performance, and accessible enough for less experienced users required significant iteration. The A/B data confirmed we got there.

What I'd do differently: I underestimated how much the testing environment matters for hardware-adjacent interfaces. Usability sessions in a studio chair don't fully capture the physical reality of standing at a console during a live show — hands slightly less stable, attention divided, lighting unpredictable. Earlier in-context testing, even informal, would have surfaced the touch target sizing issue sooner.

I'd also push harder at the start for a shared definition of "success" with engineering — aligning on what adoption rate actually means for an embedded interface, and how to measure it meaningfully, took longer than it should have.

Shipped Features

Touch EQ graph with direct node manipulation · Bottom-anchored parameter controls · Swipe-based channel navigation · Custom preset save & recall · Visual level indicators with colour-coded states

Success Metrics Defined
